````content
The irony of artificial intelligence (AI) is that it inspires new perspectives on the human experience and the nature of human intelligence. These insights are then applied to the development of human-like intelligence in machines.

Since Claude Shannon's, Seymour Papert's, and others' pioneering work in artificial intelligence, educational technology, and information theory, researchers at MIT have been at the forefront of inventing a future in artificial intelligence that is still evolving today. The current research presented in this exhibition stands on the shoulders of that early work, and spans computer science, mechanical engineering, neuroscience, and the social sciences.

The more this research explores the link between human and machine intelligence, the more it exposes the gap between the two. The ubiquitous presence of this technology, from AI in the home to robots in the workplace, compels us to ask questions about its potential and its risks, and about machine ability versus human ability.

What has become clear is that the more we advance artificial intelligence and machine learning technology, the more we need to mind the gap.

MA-3 Arm with Belgrade Hand

Marvin Minsky, a pioneer in artificial intelligence research and co-founder of the MIT Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, designed this robotic arm with the aim of developing a computer system that could see and independently locate, grasp, and manipulate objects.

Fabricated by William Bennett and used between 1965 and 1972, this "Tentacle Arm" could be controlled by a digital PDP-6 computer, was powered by hydraulics, had twelve joints (8 degrees of freedom), and was strong enough to lift a person. This work inspired Minsky's seminal 1986 book The Society of Mind, which explored the nature of intelligence and the diversity of mechanisms involved in thought, memory, and language.

panel_title: The Coin Toss
panel_id: the-coin-toss

```content
Can machines think?

Claude Shannon, a pioneer of the information age, was instrumental in the origins and development of what would become the internet. He began with a modest idea—the simplest form of information could be found in a coin toss. This piece of information could be transmitted as a 1, or a 0, or a combination of multiple 1s and 0s.

Shannon joined Bell Labs during World War II and focused on ways to improve communication technology. His first assignment in the top-secret field of cryptography gave him new insights that contributed to his revolutionary work A Mathematical Theory of Communication in 1948. An unconventional researcher and thinker, Shannon often explored new ideas by building gadgets and what he called "thinking machines," and shortly thereafter he turned his attention to the challenge of building a machine that could play chess.

Decades before IBM's Deep Blue, Shannon's Endgame not only proved that computers could play chess, but it also helped him refine his ideas about artificial intelligence and the human-machine relationship.

In 1950, Claude Shannon published the groundbreaking paper "Programming a Computer for Playing Chess." About this time, he invented a chess machine called Endgame. The name refers to the machine's ability to manage a maximum of six pieces, so he focused its computing power on the "endgame," or the final moves of a chess game. Going through dozens of relay operations for each play, including a random element that changed its move in certain circumstances, Endgame could respond to an opponent's move in roughly 10 seconds.

The Language of Intelligence

Can intelligent machines recreate everyday human behaviors?

The use of tools is innately connected to human language and intelligence. People use tools to interact with the world and to apply knowledge about their surroundings into actions and problem solving.

An inherent challenge to modeling tool use for machine learning is that it involves the physical manipulation of tangible objects. Cognitive researchers are addressing this challenge by building and studying virtual environments. Although they've found that this type of model can predict human behavior, it is difficult for an AI system, without human-like knowledge of the world, to recreate that behavior even if it is trained on masses of data.

Multisensory Machines

There's art in how we use our hands, whether tying a knot or holding a pen. You can't be proficient at something until it can be done unconsciously. This is where robotics and human abilities meet.
– Alberto Rodriguez, MIT Associate Professor, Mechanical Engineering

What does it mean for a robot to truly perceive its world?

Most people think chess is a more difficult game than Jenga, but for a machine Jenga is the greater challenge. Chess is a strategy game with no hidden information—players know what moves they can make, and they can anticipate the possible outcomes. Jenga on the other hand is a game about the subtle awareness of whether a structure is stable or not, and this can only be experienced while interacting with the structure. Machines are more skilled at computing than experiencing sensory inputs such as touch. For these reasons, it is more difficult to train a machine to play Jenga than to play chess.

Accomplishing this requires the processing of multisensory input, and combining the visual awareness of an object moving with the tactile experience of producing that motion. Merging those two experiences, visual and tactile, is important for manipulating a piece in the game of Jenga— just as it is for the many things we do with our own hands.

Joining Forces

Our work converges to a single goal of harnessing the relative strengths of humans and robots to accomplish what neither can do alone.
– Julie Shah, Professor, MIT Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics, and Lead, Interactive Robotics Group

Can robots efficiently adapt and respond to human behaviors so that people will accept them as teammates?

Interactive robots need to be able to emulate and adapt to the qualities and behaviors of human teammates. Creating an effective person-robot collaboration requires an understanding of artificial intelligence, systems engineering, and human factors such as cognition and social behaviors.

Programming robots to become effective teammates is difficult precisely because it's challenging to model human behavior. Robots need to react, assess outcomes, and carry out ideal actions, all in a timely manner that feels nonintrusive and natural to a person.

An AI-enabled robot can continually refine its plans for working with people and adapt its support to evolving and unpredictable human behavior. Although there is no single technique or algorithm to deal with all of these parameters, the only constant is that robots will always be working in the world of people.

```content
Robot Reasoning

Can a robot reason with you?

When collaborating with people, robots need to reason out their environment and the behavior and needs of the people in it. Reasoning requires a combination of - perception of elements related to the task, humans, and the environment; comprehension of their meaning as related to the robots' and humans' goals; and planning for the robot to decide what to do and when.

Verbal instructions and the context in which they are spoken can help robots reason. Where was that person when that comment was made? At what volume did the person speak? At what stage of the task did the person make the comment?

AI-enabled robots can then combine contextual understanding and planning skills to assess outcomes of different actions and make the best choice. As robots take this a step further, they can reason about people's intentions and improve their ability to collaborate.

panel_title: AlterEgo
panel_id: alter-ego

```content
A Second Self

How can we move away from AI as devices that are external to us, toward AI becoming more closely integrated with us?

Without disrupting speech or hearing, and without invading your thoughts, the AlterEgo device registers only "silent speech"—your conscious effort to say words without speaking. This effort is detected in subtle movements of your internal speech organs like the tongue and larynx.

By reading electrical neural signals from the surface of the face and neck, AlterEgo uses an AI system trained to detect and recognize words silently spoken. The AI acts on the user's instructions, then responds with a voice of its own. It conducts its auditory responses through the bone between the inner ear and skull. This process means the entire interface between the AI and the user is private and internal to that person—in a way, a cognitive extension of the individual.

The technology has the potential to augment people's everyday activities, from real-time translation that enables multi-lingual conversation, to augmenting memory and allowing people with speech-related disorders like ALS to communicate.

The First Social Robot

"Hello, Kismet."

In the late 1990s, the robotics field was moving from a longstanding focus on autonomous robots meant to work separately from people, to a focus on social robots that collaborate and interact with people as part of everyday life.

Cynthia Breazeal, now Professor of Media Arts and Sciences and lead of the Personal Robots group at MIT, developed Kismet for her doctoral research. It was the world's first social robot, designed to interact in an interpersonal way using facial expressions, vocalizations, and movements that mimic natural human mannerisms. The research was inspired by child developmental psychology and supported a more intuitive and natural interaction where the robot and the person could interpret and respond in real time to expressive cues from the other.

Professor Breazeal has since become a pioneer in the field of social robotics and what it means for us to "live with AI."

panel_title: Manipulating Media Content
panel_id: manipulating-media-content

```content
Altered Reality

Deepfakes blend imagination with the physical world. We all need to be discerning interpreters.
– D. Fox Harrell, Founding Director, MIT Center for Advanced Virtuality

Where is the line between internet meme and dangerous manipulation?

More than simply altering a video, AI and deep-learning algorithms can create a seamlessly manipulated version of the original. While this may be used in benign ways, for example in Hollywood moviemaking, other "deepfakes" are created for nefarious reasons.

From video dialogue replacement to fictional photos created from scratch, people can use AI to manipulate media in many ways. Face-swapping is one kind of video deepfake in which one person's face appears on another person's body. People create these videos by combining facial recognition algorithms and deep-learning computer models.

To swap faces in a video, one of these models is trained on many video frames of the original person's face. It encodes that person's unique features into a mathematical representation and then decodes this representation back into an image. Another model performs similarly for a second person's face.

Combining the encoder used to turn the original person's face into a simpler mathematical representation of itself, with the decoder used to turn a representation of the second person's face into an image, results in the second person's face appearing in the original video—and a potentially dangerous deepfake.

panel_title: Black Box experience - Seeing Inside the Black Box
panel_id: seeing-inside-the-black-box

```content
Neural networks, loosely modeled on the function of the human brain, consist of thousands or millions of small interconnected processing nodes, and they are trained on massive amounts of data. Each node receives data from other nodes in previous layers of code and sends data to other nodes in subsequent layers of code.

Using numbers as an example, it's safe to say the human brain can generally recognize a written number, regardless of fuzzy imaging or bad handwriting. But when we input an image of that number into a neural network intended to learn handwritten input, it becomes exceedingly more difficult.

If your input is 100 pixels square, there are 10,000 pixels, or nodes, to process. Each one holds a weight based on how clearly it is part of the written number input into the first layer of code. Based on those weights, subsequent layers activate with increasing precision, as nodes activate based on curved or straight parts of the digit, or how straight lines are angled. The final layer of just ten nodes, each representing a number, then "chooses" the option determined to be the correct answer.

```content
Smaller than a piece of confetti, this brain-on-a-chip processes information in a way that mimics the human brain's neural network. panel_title: The Meaning of Data
panel_id: the-meaning-of-data

```content
If we want to talk about innovation, we have to talk about domination.
– Chelsea Barabas, Research scientist, MIT Media, Arts and Sciences, Comparative Media Studies

Sometimes issues of algorithmic fairness can be improved by training AI systems on more data. Sometimes, however, adding more data, even with intentions of increasing accuracy and decreasing bias, can have the opposite effect.

Oftentimes, designers of AI systems treat white, male profiles as the default "universal user." As a result, they train systems like facial recognition on datasets containing more light-skinned male faces than dark-skinned female faces. This overrepresentation of white male faces in data can have dangerous implications for equity when it comes to certain consumer applications, such as computer vision for self-driving cars. But diversifying data sets is not always the solution. For some populations, increased data accuracy only serves to perpetuate harm when, for example, it is embedded in AI in surveillance tools used by law enforcement.

Training AI systems on more data will increase people's visibility in automated systems, whether in voice recognition systems that identify individual speakers or in predictive systems that determine loan eligibility. Across race, gender, and economic lines, this visibility will result in a more seamless consumer experience for some. For others, it will result in increased vulnerability to harm. It is this disparity that needs to be recognized in order to be averted.

As a masters student at the MIT Media Lab's Center for Civic Media, Joy Buolamwini discovered that facial detection software located her face better when she was wearing a white mask similar to this one.
````
